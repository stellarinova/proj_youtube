Get Rid of Your Guilt

I want to ask you a very personal question and I hope this isn't too direct but how many of you have ever been caught doing something wrong?

When we're caught doing something wrong, there are two different responses: worldly sorrow and godly sorrow. Worldly sorrow says "I'm sorry I got caught" but doesn't lead to real change. Godly sorrow, however, leads to repentance—a genuine turning away from sin toward God. As 2 Corinthians 7 tells us, godly sorrow brings repentance that leads to salvation and leaves no regret, while worldly sorrow brings death.

The key difference? Worldly sorrow is regretting the consequences; godly sorrow is regretting the sin itself because it breaks God's heart. One focuses on self-preservation, the other on relationship restoration.

Understanding What Sin Really Is

Sin isn't just breaking rules—it's missing the mark. From the Greek word "hamartia" (an archery term meaning to miss the target), sin is falling short of God's glorious standard. When we believe there is a God who sets absolute truth (personified in Jesus as "the way, the truth, and the life"), missing His standard is what we call sin.

Our culture often tries to sanitize or rebrand sin—calling lies "white lies" or exaggerations, calling sexual immorality "meeting my needs," or saying "I'm living my truth" to avoid accountability. But when we minimize sin, we minimize our need for a Savior. Until we see ourselves as sinners, we won't see our need for salvation.

The Story of Young Stephen and the Rattlesnake

The pastor shares a personal story about his three-year-old son Steven (nicknamed "Buie") who was delighted playing with a rattlesnake, treating it as a friend. When the father disposed of the dangerous snake, the boy was devastated—not because he understood the danger, but because he lost his "friend." This illustrates how we often befriend sins that are actually dangerous to us, rationalizing them as harmless when they're truly destructive.

We befriend sins like jealousy ("I'm jealous of her perfect hair and kids"), materialism ("I just like nice things"), or cheating ("I'm just getting by"). We make excuses, blame others, and minimize the impact—all signs our hearts are growing cold toward God. Worldly sorrow may make us feel bad about getting caught, but it lacks genuine repentance.

Godly Sorrow: Heartfelt Brokenness Over Sin

Godly sorrow is very different—it's a deep, honest, sincere brokenness over our sinfulness. It's marked by genuine humility and grief not because we were caught, but because we sinned against God. This leads to heartfelt repentance: turning from sin and running to God with our whole hearts.

True repentance isn't just feeling bad that others know our sin—it's feeling broken that we've broken God's heart. It's not a temporary "I'm sorry I'll probably do it again tomorrow" but a wholehearted turning away from destructive patterns and toward God's higher calling.

A Mark of Spiritual Maturity

One mark of maturity when walking in the Spirit is immediate awareness of sin. When we're distant from God—ignoring Him, neglecting His Word—we can slip into sinfulness, justify it, rationalize it, and almost accept it. But when God's Word is in our heart and His Spirit directs our steps, the moment we step out of His will, we're immediately convicted (not condemned) that there's a better way.

This conviction leads us to confess quickly—"Oh God, I'm sorry, I didn't mean to"—and then repent wholeheartedly. We don't wait weeks to rededicate; we turn from sin immediately, slam the door on temptation, and run toward God's higher truth.

What Happens When We Confess to God?

Scripture is clear: if we confess our sins, God is faithful and just to forgive us our sins and purify us from all unrighteousness. He doesn't hold our sins against us or bring condemnation into our lives. Instead, He freely forgives us—not because we deserve it, but because of His grace—and He cleanses our hearts when we confess.

We don't earn forgiveness through our remorse or penance; we receive it as a gift of grace. When we receive this undeserving mercy, it changes us—we become different people, not because we've performed well, but because we've encountered God's transformative grace.

Experiencing God's Kindness That Leads to Repentance

God's approach to our sin isn't anger or wrath—it's kindness, tolerance, and patience. As Romans 2:4 reveals, it's God's kindness that's intended to turn us from our sin, not His judgment. His love and grace lead us to repentance, making it safe for us to confess and turn away from sin.

This is why the habit of godly sorrow—confessing quickly and repenting wholeheartedly—is so vital. The devil doesn't want us to adopt this habit because if we do, we won't live in shame, we won't stay distant from God, and we'll be able to fulfill our calling. Godly sorrow leads to salvation with no regret.

The Invitation: Turn from Sin and Turn to Jesus

God's invitation today is clear: don't settle for anything less than His best. If you want to be sensitive and righteous, honor God by lifting up your heart to Him. If you need His grace and forgiveness, step away from your sin and give your life to Jesus—letting Him be your Lord, Savior, and King.

When we call on Jesus, He hears our prayers, forgives every sin (past, present, and future), and makes us brand new. This isn't a little Sunday school prayer—it's a life-changing moment where we turn from the old life of sin and pursue Jesus with our whole hearts at all of our churches.

Today can be your moment to turn away from the weight of sin you've carried too long. Recognize that it's impossible to work your way to God—we're made right with Him only by His grace through faith in Jesus, who died for our sins and rose again. When we turn from sin and turn to Jesus, we're not just changing behavior—we're experiencing new life.
