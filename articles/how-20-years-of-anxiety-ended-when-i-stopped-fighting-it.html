How 20 Years of Anxiety Ended When I Stopped Fighting It

A personal journey from chronic anxiety to peace through acceptance, not more fixes

I dealt with anxiety for 20 years. I could not remember a time when it wasn't a huge problem for me. My stress tolerance had always been non-existent. It got especially bad after my first son was born in 2015 with hip dysplasia, requiring a brace for an entire year—a period so anxiety-provoking that I ended up seeing a doctor who put me on antipsychotics because I couldn't sleep and kept hearing my son cry when he wasn't.

For two decades, I was committed to finding an easier solution. I literally tried everything that claimed to help anxiety: therapy, medications, supplements, health OCD approaches—you name it. Some things helped temporarily, but never long-term. The anxiety always came back, usually worse.

Then, after about 20 years, something counterintuitive finally worked. It wasn't a new supplement, therapy technique, or medication. The breakthrough came when I stopped looking for fixes altogether.

The Difference Between Dysregulation and Sensitization

I learned there's a crucial distinction between nervous system dysregulation and sensitization:

  • Dysregulation: Your body can deal with threats and come back to baseline, just maybe a little slower
  • Sensitization: Your body gets stuck in threat response, interpreting everything as dangerous—even safe things like switching lanes, opening doors, or leaves falling

With sensitization, it's like a car alarm going off for any purpose at all, even when there's no actual threat. Your nervous system is working too well—it's doing what it was designed to do, but in overdrive, assuming everything is dangerous to keep you protected.

The Real Solution: Acceptance, Not More Fixes

The thing that finally healed me was the scariest option: I had to let go. Not hold on tighter. Not seek more reassurance. Not Google more symptoms. Not try one more therapy or supplement.

Real healing came from:

  1. Accepting the situation: Deciding I was done trying to make it work and allowing my symptoms to exist without fighting them
  2. Allowing uncertainty: Learning to be with the discomfort of not knowing, rather than seeking certainty through endless research and reassurance-seeking
  3. Changing my response: Instead of fighting anxiety, I started responding to it with less fear
  4. Doing less, not more: The healing wasn't in adding another tool, but in releasing my grip on control

Why Fixing Symptoms Doesn't Work

For years, I missed the message my anxiety was trying to tell me. When we just eliminate symptoms without addressing the root, we're not getting to what the symptoms are actually trying to communicate.

My anxiety wasn't the problem—it was a messenger. The real issue was my resistance to reality and my inability to tolerate uncertainty. Every time I sought reassurance or tried to "fix" my anxiety, I was reinforcing the belief that I couldn't handle uncertainty, which actually increased my fear over time.

The Path Forward

If you've struggled with chronic anxiety for years and nothing has worked—despite being an "A+ student" in therapy, trying every program, bringing your therapist notes—there might not be anything actually wrong with you. Your system might simply be sensitized.

The good news? Sensitization can be reversed. Thousands of people are doing it. The solution isn't finding another fix—it's changing your relationship with anxiety and uncertainty.

You don't need to believe fully that you'll be okay, but you can't be in 100% doubt all the time either. Healing begins when you're willing to say, "I don't know," and actually be okay with not knowing.

On the other side of accepting your symptoms and allowing uncertainty, there's a life waiting with very low levels of fear—not because there's nothing to fear, but because you've learned to distinguish between real threats and false alarms.

Today, I wake up feeling peaceful, happy, inspired, hopeful, optimistic, and excited to create and help others. The body scan of doom is gone. I drink coffee now (sometimes two cups a day!) without fear. This isn't about managing anxiety—it's about fundamentally changing who you are in relation to your experience.

If this resonates with you, know that you're not broken. You don't need another fix. You need to stop fighting and start allowing. The anxiety has been waiting for you to hear its message.
