Psychologist: 5 Anxiety Rules to Stop Avoiding Life

Anxiety doesn't get smaller—your life gets bigger first, and that's where the real work begins

Anxiety doesn't get smaller. Your life gets bigger first. I'm a psychologist. I've treated anxiety for over 15 years and I've had it much longer than that. I'm not the guy who beat it. I still struggle with anxiety, but I've learned to live with it in a way that doesn't paralyze me.

Rule 1: Anxiety Doesn't Get Smaller—Your Life Gets Bigger First

Most people approach anxiety backwards. They try to make their anxiety smaller before they start living their lives. But that's not how it works. Anxiety doesn't get smaller until you start doing the things that make you anxious. Your life has to get bigger first—you have to start doing the things you're avoiding—and then your anxiety will gradually decrease as a side effect of living fully.

Rule 2: The Anxiety Loop

Anxiety creates a vicious cycle: you feel anxious, so you avoid the thing that makes you anxious, which gives you temporary relief, but then you feel more anxious about avoiding it, which makes you want to avoid it more, and the cycle continues. The key to breaking this cycle is to do the thing you're avoiding while feeling anxious, not after the anxiety goes away.

Rule 3: Practice Feeling Anxious on Purpose

One of the most effective ways to reduce anxiety's power over you is to practice feeling anxious on purpose. This might sound counterintuitive, but when you deliberately expose yourself to anxiety-provoking situations and stay with the discomfort, you teach your brain that anxiety is not dangerous. You're not trying to eliminate the anxiety—you're changing your relationship with it.

Rule 4: Start Small and Build Tolerance

You don't have to jump into the deepest end of the pool right away. Start with small, manageable exposures to anxiety-provoking situations and gradually work your way up. This builds what psychologists call "distress tolerance"—your ability to withstand uncomfortable feelings without needing to escape or avoid them.

Rule 5: Focus on Values, Not Feelings

Instead of asking yourself, "How do I feel?" ask yourself, "What matters to me?" When you focus on your values—what's important to you in life—you create a compass that guides your actions regardless of how you're feeling. Anxiety might be loud, but your values can be louder if you let them guide you.

The good news is that anxiety is highly treatable. You don't have to eliminate anxiety completely to live a full, meaningful life. You just need to learn how to coexist with it in a way that doesn't let it dictate your choices. Every time you do something that matters to you while feeling anxious, you're rewiring your brain and building evidence that you can handle discomfort.

Remember: anxiety is not a stop sign—it's a yield sign. It's telling you to slow down and pay attention, not to turn around and go back. When you treat anxiety as information rather than a command, you regain your power to choose how you respond to it.
